Core Mathematical Formulas

These are the exact equations Prof. Daigle prints on the exam formula sheet and expects you to use. For each one: the formula, what every symbol means, and the World Cup reason you'd reach for it. The exam gives you the formulas — your job is knowing which to grab and how to feed data into it.

A. Describing one variable (Univariate)

Mean Average goals per match

Population: μ = ΣXiN   ·   Sample: x̄ = Σxin
Σ"add them all up" (sum)
Xieach individual score (e.g., each match's goal count)
N / nnumber of cases in the population (N) vs a sample (n)
Add up every goal Brazil scored across all 7 games of a World Cup and divide by 7 to get their average goals per match. It's the center of gravity for a team's scoring.

Variance & Std. Deviation How streaky is a team?

s2 = Σ(xi − x̄)2n − 1     s = √ s2 
s2sample variance — the average squared distance from the mean
ssample standard deviation — variance back in normal units (goals, not goals²)
xi−x̄how far one match's goals sit from the team's average (a "deviation")
n − 1degrees of freedom for a sample. Use n−1 for a sample, N for a whole population.
Two teams can both average 2.0 goals a game, but Germany scores 2, 2, 2, 2 (rock steady) while Brazil scores 0, 4, 1, 3 (boom or bust). Standard deviation is the number that captures that consistency. A scout comparing reliability of two strikers wants the one with the lower s.
Classic trap: dividing by n instead of n−1 for a sample. The exam loves this — see Quiz Q5.

Z-score Standardizing a performance

Z = X − μσ   (raw score)  ·   z = x̄ − μsx̄   (sample mean)
Znumber of standard deviations a value sits above (+) or below (−) the mean
Xthe raw score you're standardizing
μ, σthe population mean and standard deviation
A striker bangs in 14 league goals when the league averages μ = 8 with σ = 3. His Z = (14−8)/3 = +2.0 — he's 2 SDs above average, better than ~97.7% of players (read that % off the Z-table). Z-scores let you compare a defender's tackles to a striker's goals on one common ruler.

B. From sample to population (Inference)

Standard Error & Confidence Interval Estimating the true mean

sx̄ = s√n     CI = x̄ ± z · s√n
sx̄standard error of the mean — how much sample means bounce around the true mean
zthe confidence multiplier: 1.645 (90%), 1.96 (95%), 2.57 (99%)
± termthe margin of error (MOE)
You clock the sprint speed of 36 randomly sampled World Cup wingers and get a mean of 32.4 km/h. The CI says: "I'm 95% confident the true average speed of all wingers falls in 32.4 ± MOE." Bigger n ⇒ smaller standard error ⇒ tighter, more useful estimate.

Proportions & Sample Size Polling the fans

sp = √p(1−p)n  ·  CI = p ± z·sp  ·  n = Z2 · p(1−p)MOE2
pthe sample proportion (e.g., 0.56 of fans favor a bigger tournament)
spstandard error of the proportion
MOEthe margin of error you're willing to accept (e.g., 0.02 = ±2%)
nthe sample size you'd need to hit that MOE at that confidence
A poll of 803 fans finds 56% want a 48-team World Cup. Build a 95% CI around 56% to claim where the true fan-base opinion lies. The sample-size formula answers the flip side: "how many fans must I survey to be within ±2%?"

C. Two categorical variables (Chi-Square family)

Chi-Square (χ²) Do two categories go together?

χ2 = Σ (fo − fe)2fe   ·   fe = row total × column totalN   ·   df = (r−1)(c−1)
foobserved frequency — the count actually in a cell
feexpected frequency — the count if there were no relationship
r, cnumber of rows and columns in the crosstab
dfdegrees of freedom — you need it to look up the critical value
Is group-stage outcome (Advance / Eliminated) related to a team's continental federation (UEFA / CONMEBOL / CAF …)? Both variables are categories, so you count how many teams fall in each combination and ask whether the pattern departs from pure chance. Bigger gap between observed and expected ⇒ bigger χ².

Cramér's V & Phi (Φ) How strong is that link?

V = √χ2n(k−1)     Φ = √χ2n
kthe smaller of (rows, columns) — so k−1 is the smaller dimension minus 1
ntotal sample size
Φphi — only valid for a 2×2 table (a dichotomy). Otherwise use V.
χ² only tells you whether a relationship is real (significance). It cannot tell you how strong it is because a huge sample inflates it. Cramér's V rescales χ² to a clean 0–1 strength score. V ≈ 0.35 between federation and yellow-card discipline = a moderate tendency. Ranges: ~0.1 weak, ~0.3 moderate, ~0.5+ strong.
Ordinal cousins: if both variables are ordinal (ranked), use tau-b (τb) instead — it also shows direction (+/−). Avoid Gamma (over-inflates) and never use Lambda (reads 0 whenever the modal category repeats).

D. Comparing group averages (t-test & ANOVA)

Independent-Samples t-test Two groups, equal variances

sx̄1−x̄2 = √ (n1−1)s12 + (n2−1)s22n1+n2−2 · n1+n2n1n2
t = x̄1 − x̄2sx̄1−x̄2   ·   df = n1 + n2 − 2
x̄₁,x̄₂the two group means (e.g., possession % of winners vs losers)
s₁²,s₂²the two group variances
n₁,n₂the two group sample sizes
ttest statistic — compare to the critical t at your df
Do winning teams really hold more possession than losing teams? You have two groups and one interval DV (possession %), so the t-test asks whether the gap between the two averages is too big to be random.
Equal-variance check: if one group's variance is more than ~2× the other's, you can not assume equal variances. The exam asks this directly.

ANOVA (F-test) Three or more groups

MSbetween = SSbetweenk−1  ·  MSwithin = SSwithinN−k  ·  F = MSbetweenMSwithin
knumber of groups (e.g., 4 confederations)
Ntotal number of cases across all groups
SSsum of squares — between groups (signal) vs within groups (noise)
Fratio of signal to noise; look it up with df = (k−1, N−k)
A t-test compares two groups; when you have three or more — say average goals per match across UEFA, CONMEBOL, CAF, and AFC — you use ANOVA. A big F means the differences between confederations dwarf the random noise within each one. A follow-up Tukey test then says which specific pairs differ.

E. Two numeric variables (Correlation & Regression)

Pearson's r Do two stats move together?

sxy = Σ(xi−x̄)(yi−ȳ)n−1   ·   r = sxysx · sy   ·   r2
sxycovariance — do X and Y rise and fall together?
rcorrelation, from −1 to +1. Sign = direction, size = strength.
r2share of variation in Y explained by X (e.g., r=0.6 ⇒ r²=0.36 = 36%)
Does a team's passing accuracy % track with its shots on target? An r near +1 means sharper passing reliably comes with more shots; r near 0 means no linear link. Both variables must be interval/ratio.

OLS Regression Drawing the line of best fit

β (slope) = SSxySSx  ·  α (intercept) = ȳ − βx̄
Bivariate: Ŷ = α + βX   ·   Multivariate: Ŷ = α + β1X1 + … + βnXn
Ŷthe predicted value of the dependent variable ("Y-hat")
αintercept — predicted Y when X = 0 (where the line crosses the Y-axis)
βslope — how much Y changes for each 1-unit rise in X
SSxy, SSxsums of squares (the Σ pieces, not divided by n−1)
Predict a nation's World Cup goals (Y) from its football budget (X). Regression fits the single straight line that sits closest to all the country dots. Then you can forecast: "a country spending $150M is predicted to score Ŷ goals." The full worked version is Walkthrough 2 below.

🟨 Walkthrough 1 — Chi-Square: Are South American teams "dirtier"?

You suspect continental federation is related to yellow-card discipline. You sample 140 teams and record each as a Clean, Average, or Dirty team by cards drawn, split by UEFA (Europe) vs CONMEBOL (South America). Test at 95%+ confidence.
1

State the hypotheses.

Ha: A team's federation is related to its card discipline (South American teams draw more cards than European teams).
H0: There is no relationship between federation and card discipline.

2

Lay out the observed counts (fo) with row & column totals.

Compute expected counts: fe = (row total × column total) / N.

Clean·UEFA = (55×80)/140 = 31.43  |  Clean·CONMEBOL = (55×60)/140 = 23.57
Avg·UEFA = (50×80)/140 = 28.57  |  Avg·CONMEBOL = (50×60)/140 = 21.43
Dirty·UEFA = (35×80)/140 = 20.00  |  Dirty·CONMEBOL = (35×60)/140 = 15.00

4

Build the χ² table: for each cell compute (fo − fe)² / fe.

Compare to the critical value. On the χ² table at df = 2: the 0.05 cutoff is 5.991, the 0.01 cutoff is 9.210, and the 0.001 cutoff is 13.816. Our χ² = 17.29 clears even the 0.001 column.

Measure the strength (χ² alone can't). Federation is nominal, so use Cramér's V with k = min(3,2) = 2:

V = √(17.29 / [140 × (2−1)]) = √(17.29/140) = √0.1235 = 0.35

χ² = 17.29, df = 2 → significant at >99.9% confidence, so we reject H₀. Cramér's V = 0.35 signals a moderate relationship. Reading the table, CONMEBOL teams are far more likely to be "Dirty" (25 observed vs 15 expected) while UEFA teams skew "Clean" — evidence supporting Ha.

🎽 Walkthrough 2 — t-test: Do winners really keep the ball more?

You compare average possession % for 10 winning teams (x̄₁ = 58.4, s₁ = 6.2) against 8 losing teams (x̄₂ = 49.1, s₂ = 5.4). Independent samples, equal variances assumed.
1

Ha: Winning teams have higher average possession than losing teams.   H0: No difference in mean possession between winners and losers.

2

Difference in means: 58.4 − 49.1 = 9.3 percentage points.

3

Square the SDs into variances first: s₁² = 6.2² = 38.44  ·  s₂² = 5.4² = 29.16.

4

Standard error of the difference — plug into the pooled formula:

= √{ [(10−1)(38.44) + (8−1)(29.16)] / (10+8−2) × (10+8)/(10×8) }
= √{ [9(38.44) + 7(29.16)] / 16 × 18/80 }
= √{ [345.96 + 204.12] / 16 × 0.225 }
= √{ (550.08/16) × 0.225 } = √{ 34.38 × 0.225 } = √7.7355 = 2.781

5

t statistic: t = 9.3 / 2.781 = 3.34.

6

Degrees of freedom: df = n₁ + n₂ − 2 = 10 + 8 − 2 = 16.

7

Critical value (two-tail t table, df = 16): 0.05 → 2.120, 0.01 → 2.921, 0.001 → 4.015. Our t = 3.34 exceeds the 0.01 cutoff (2.921) but not 0.001.

8

Should you have assumed equal variances? Ratio = 38.44 / 29.16 = 1.32, which is under 2 — so yes, equal variances is a safe assumption here.

t = 3.34, df = 16 → clears the 99% threshold, so we reject H₀. Winners hold significantly more possession (58.4% vs 49.1%), and with the variance ratio under 2 the equal-variance version of the test was appropriate.

📈 Walkthrough 5 — Reading a Multiple-Regression Table (α, β, p, R²)

SPSS ran a model predicting a national team's FIFA ranking points (Y) from three predictors. Interpret the output — no calculation needed, just correct reading. This is exactly the "interpret the printout" section on every final.
Predictorβ (slope)p-valueVerdict at α = 0.05
Avg. possession %+4.200.001Significant ✓
Avg. goals conceded−9.800.004Significant ✓
Days of travel to host−1.100.312Not significant ✗
Intercept (α)310.00.000—
Model: F(3, 96) = 22.5, p < 0.001  |  R² = 0.41  |  n = 100

① Fit of the model — use F and R²

F(3, 96) = 22.5 with p < 0.001 means you can be >99.9% confident the model as a whole is a real predictor of ranking points (very low chance of a Type I error). R² = 0.41 means 41% of the variation in ranking points is explained by these three predictors together.

② The intercept (α = 310)

The predicted ranking points for a team with 0% possession, 0 goals conceded, and 0 travel days. Often not physically meaningful — it just anchors the line — but it's the starting number in the prediction equation.

③ Each slope (β) — significance, then degree, then form

  • Possession (β = +4.20, p = 0.001): significant at >99% confidence. Form is positive; degree: each extra 1% of possession predicts +4.2 ranking points, holding the others constant.
  • Goals conceded (β = −9.80, p = 0.004): significant. Negative form — each additional goal conceded per match predicts a 9.8-point drop. Defense matters most here.
  • Travel days (β = −1.10, p = 0.312): p > 0.05, so not significant — we fail to reject its null. Even though the sign is negative, we can't claim travel matters; don't interpret its size further.

④ Write the equation & predict

Ŷ = 310 + 4.20(possession) − 9.80(goals conceded) − 1.10(travel days)

For a team with 60% possession, 1.0 goal conceded, 3 travel days:
Ŷ = 310 + 4.20(60) − 9.80(1) − 1.10(3) = 310 + 252 − 9.8 − 3.3 = 548.9 ranking points.

Golden rule for every coefficient: check significance first (is p < α?). Only if it's significant do you go on to describe form (sign of β) and degree (size of β). A non-significant predictor gets no further interpretation.

"Which Statistical Test Do I Use?"

The single most valuable exam skill: reading the levels of measurement of the two variables and instantly naming the right test. Nominal = unordered categories (federation, PR-system yes/no). Ordinal = ranked categories (low/med/high). Interval/Ratio = real numbers (goals, %, points). Here's the decision matrix.

TestIndependent Var (IV)Dependent Var (DV)World Cup trigger example
Chi-Square Nominal / Ordinal Nominal / Ordinal Group-stage outcome (Advance/Out) × Continental federation. Both are categories → count cells, test χ².
t-test Nominal — exactly 2 groups (a dichotomy) Interval / Ratio Home vs Away status → average distance traveled (km). Two groups, one numeric average to compare.
ANOVA (F) Nominal — 3+ groups Interval / Ratio Tournament stage reached (Group/R16/QF/SF/Final) or coach's nationality → average goals per match.
Correlation (r) Interval / Ratio Interval / Ratio Passing accuracy % ↔ shots on target. Two numeric stats — do they move together? (No IV/DV direction needed.)
OLS Regression Interval / Ratio Interval / Ratio Passing accuracy % (X) predicting shots on target (Y). Same data as r, but now you want a predictive line (α, β).

Rapid-fire decision flow

Q1 — Is my dependent variable a number (goals, %, points)?
➡ No (it's a category, like Advance/Eliminated): you're in Chi-Square land. Then pick the association measure by level ↓
➡ Yes (it's interval/ratio): go to Q2.
Q2 — What's my independent variable?
➡ A group label with 2 categories → t-test (Winners vs Losers).
➡ A group label with 3+ categories → ANOVA (goals by confederation).
➡ Another number → Correlation / Regression (budget → goals).
Q3 (inside Chi-Square) — which strength/association measure?
➡ At least one variable nominal → Cramér's V (or Phi if the table is 2×2).
➡ Both variables ordinal → tau-b (τb) — it also gives direction.
➡ Never use Lambda (= 0 whenever the DV's modal category repeats across IV levels). Avoid Gamma — it over-inflates.
Q4 — Correlation or Regression?
➡ Just "do they move together, and how strongly?" → Pearson's r.
➡ "Use X to predict/forecast Y" (you need α and β) → OLS Regression.
Level-of-measurement facts the exam checks directly: in a t-test the IV is a dichotomy (nominal) and the DV is interval/ratio. In regression, both variables must be interval/ratio. Direction of an interval/ratio relationship is read from the sign of r and the sign of β — never from r².

High-Yield Practice Quiz

Five questions pitched at the exact inference-and-calculation difficulty of the real final. Click an option to test yourself, then hit Reveal solution for the full math and why each wrong answer is wrong.

Question 1 · Referee bias & the p-value
A researcher runs a chi-square test of whether referees give more red cards to teams from outside the host's continent. SPSS reports χ² with p = 0.023, and the study used α = 0.05. What's the correct decision?
  • A.Fail to reject H₀ — 0.023 is a tiny effect, so referee bias is negligible.
  • B.Recalculate the standard error, because p is below 0.05.
  • C.Reject H₀ — there's only a 2.3% chance of a Type I error, which is below the 5% threshold.
  • D.Reject H₀, and conclude bias explains 2.3% of red cards.
✅ Correct: C

The rule is "if p < α, reject H₀." Here p = 0.023 < α = 0.05, so we reject the null and claim a real relationship, with only a 2.3% chance of a Type I error (rejecting a true null).

A confuses a p-value with effect size — p measures the risk of a false positive, not how big/small the relationship is. B is nonsense: a significant p never triggers "recompute the standard error." D misreads p as a proportion of variance explained — that's what r² does, not p.
Question 2 · Type I vs Type II error in VAR
A VAR review tests the null H₀ = "the goal is valid (no foul)." The VAR crew lets a goal stand that actually involved a foul — i.e., they failed to overturn a call they should have. In hypothesis-testing terms, this is a:
  • A.Type I error — rejecting a true null.
  • B.Type II error — failing to reject a false null.
  • C.Correct decision — the goal counted.
  • D.A sampling error, fixed by a bigger sample.
✅ Correct: B

The null ("goal is valid") is actually false (there was a foul), but the crew failed to reject it (let the goal stand). Failing to reject a false null = Type II error. The fix for Type II risk is a larger sample / more power — here, more camera angles.

A is the mirror image — a Type I error would be disallowing a valid goal (rejecting a true null). C is wrong because a foul-tainted goal counting is precisely the mistake. D confuses the two error types with random sampling error.
Question 3 · OLS prediction
A regression predicting a nation's total World Cup goals (Y) from its football budget in $ millions (X) yields Ŷ = 3.0 + 0.008·X. What are the predicted goals for a country with a $500M budget?
  • A.3.008 goals
  • B.4.0 goals
  • C.40 goals
  • D.7.0 goals
✅ Correct: D

Substitute X = 500 into the equation: Ŷ = 3.0 + 0.008(500) = 3.0 + 4.0 = 7.0 goals. The intercept (3.0) is the baseline; the slope term adds 4.0.

A adds the slope as if X = 1 (0.008 instead of 0.008×500). B reports only the slope term (4.0) and forgets to add the intercept. C multiplies 0.008×500 wrong (missed the decimal) and drops the intercept.
Question 4 · Chi-square degrees of freedom
A crosstab tests whether a team's continental federation (6 categories) is related to its group-stage result (Win / Draw / Loss — 3 categories). How many degrees of freedom does the chi-square test have?
  • A.18
  • B.10
  • C.8
  • D.5
✅ Correct: B

df = (r − 1)(c − 1). With 6 federations and 3 results: df = (6 − 1)(3 − 1) = 5 × 2 = 10.

A is r × c = 6 × 3 = 18 (forgot to subtract 1 from each). C adds instead of multiplies: (6−1)+(3−1) = 8. D only subtracted 1 from the rows (6−1) and ignored the columns entirely.
Question 5 · Sample standard deviation
Brazil scored 1, 3, 2, 4, 5 goals across a 5-match sample (mean = 3). Treating this as a sample, what is the standard deviation?
  • A.≈ 1.58
  • B.2.5
  • C.≈ 1.41
  • D.2.0
✅ Correct: A

Deviations from the mean of 3: (−2, 0, −1, +1, +2). Squared: 4, 0, 1, 1, 4 → Σ = 10. For a sample, divide by n − 1 = 4: s² = 10/4 = 2.5. Then s = √2.5 = 1.58.

B stopped at the variance (2.5) and forgot the square root. C divided by n = 5 (the population formula): √(10/5) = 1.41 — the exam's favorite trap. D is just a rough guess with no basis in the formula.

Exam-Day Game Plan

  • Budget time by weight. A section worth 20% deserves ~20% of your 2h45m. Read the whole exam first, then attack.
  • Always SHOW ALL WORK. Partial credit is huge — write the formula, then plug in, then solve. A right answer with no work can lose points; a wrong answer with correct setup earns most of them.
  • For every test, hit the trio: significance → degree → form. Is it real (p vs α / critical value)? How strong (V, r, r², β)? Which direction (sign of τb, r, β; or which category is higher)?
  • Variance before standard error. In t-tests, square the SDs first — a skipped step the key explicitly flags.
  • Match the association measure to the level of measurement (Cramér's V / Phi for nominal; τb for ordinal; never Lambda; avoid Gamma).
  • Non-significant coefficient = stop. Don't interpret the size or direction of a predictor whose p > α.
  • Bring the kit: a scientific calculator with parentheses (no phones/tablets), your one double-sided 8.5×11 cheat sheet, pencils, and blank numbered paper.
